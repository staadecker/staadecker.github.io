Course overview

This course covers the mathematical foundations in statistics needed for doing PhD-level economics and econometrics. It is organized as follows:

  1. Part 1 covers the foundations of statistics, namely random variables and tools to analyze their behaviors as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞, including the delta method, the central limit theorem and O-notation. These tools will be useful to estimate our statistics for large sample sizes.
  2. Part 2 covers statistics and estimators, particularly what makes a “good” estimator and how to estimate “goodness” through tools like the Rao-Cramer bound and Fisher information.
  3. Part 3 covers statistical tests. I have not written up my notes for this part.

Familiarity with calculus, matrix algebra and other math concepts like the :taylor series expansion is important.

Part 1: Probability

It is often said that probability is the inverse of statistics[1]. While statistics is about making inferences about the world given observations, probability is about making claims as to what me will observe given a predefined world. Statistics is inductive. Probability is deductive. Statistics is backward looking. Probability is forward looking.

Although technically correct, I don’t find this definition particularly helpful. Rather, I like to think of probability as the field of pure mathematics that serves as the foundation of statistics, a field of applied mathematics. Probability tells you what to expect given a fully-defined mathematical problem. Statistics suggests how one can convert the real world into such a problem and meaningfully interpret the problem’s results. Probability is entirely built upon a :set of mathematical axioms. Statistics is built on probability with an added sprinkle of philosophy.

For example, frequentist statistics (the focus of this course) adopts of philosophical framework reminiscent of Plato’s cave. It assumes that there exists a real world containing the truth we wish to find, but we cannot directly observe this real world. Rather, we only get to observe samples drawn randomly from this real world from which we are to make inductions about the real world (aided by tools from probability). Bayesian statistics is another approach to statistics grounded in a different philosophy. In general, the application of probability to the real world is a contested and fascinating area of philosophy[2].

We’re now ready to dive into probability, the mathematical framework that will underpin the statistics discussed in Parts 2 and 3.

:x Probability spaces

Formally, probability is a subset of measure theory, a branch of mathematics that helps deal with measurable sets such as magnitudes, masses, geometrical measures like length, area, volume, and, of course, probability.

From a measure theory perspective, all probabilities originate from a probability space, a triplet of three mathematical objects: (Ω,F,P)(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)(Ω,F,P). Ω\OmegaΩ is the sample space which represents all potential outcomes (realizations) of an experiment (i.e. a single draw). Note that these realizations ω\omegaω need not be a number; ω\omegaω could be the description “the first die rolled a 5 and the second rolled a 4.” Random variables are functions that map these realizations ω\omegaω to the space of real numbers R\mathbb{R}R. In order for random variables to have a cumulative probability distribution FX(t):R→[0,1]F_X(t): \mathbb{R} \to [0,1]FX​(t):R→[0,1], measure theory introduces the concept of a probability measure PPP, a function that maps measurable sets of outcomes F\mathcal{F}F to the probability of observing an outcome in that set. Note the language: although conceptually you can think of a probability measure PPP as simply mapping outcomes ω∈Ω\omega \in \Omegaω∈Ω to the probability [0,1][0,1][0,1] of that outcome, it is actually mapping all measurable sets of outcomes F\mathcal{F}F to their probabilities. While beyond the scope of this course, F\mathcal{F}F is necessary because not all sets of Ω\OmegaΩ are measurable (see this video on Vitali sets).

To summarize, measure theory formalizes probability as follows:

  • Probabilities originate from a probability space (Ω,F,P)(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)(Ω,F,P).
  • Ω\OmegaΩ is the sample space, the space of all potential experimental outcomes.
  • ω∈Ω\omega \in \Omegaω∈Ω is any one sample or outcome.
  • Random variables are functions that map Ω→R\Omega \to \mathbb{R}Ω→R.
  • F\mathcal{F}F is the space of all measurable subsets of Ω\OmegaΩ (i.e. events).
  • The probability measure PPP maps F→[0,1]\mathcal{F} \to [0,1]F→[0,1].
  • The cumulative probability distribution FXF_XFX​ stems from combining the probability measure PPP and the random variable XXX.

To learn more, read about sigma algebras and measure theory.

Limits, convergence, and asymptotics

Later in the course, we will want to make approximations for large sample sizes. For example, we might want to argue that some random variable of our current sample XnX_nXn​ is actually well approximated by the random variable XXX when nnn is large. Such arguments can simplify our statistics since often XXX is a simpler expression to work with than XnX_nXn​.

To make such arguments, we must introduce mathematical tools to deal with asymptotics—the behavior of random variables as nnn approaches infinity (denoted n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞).

You may be familiar with limits and their :delta-epsilon formalization to describe the behavior of (non-random) numbers that approach infinity. An extension of this same concept exists for random variables.

Specifically, there are two notable ways in which a random variable XnX_nXn​ can be said to converge onto a different random variable YYY.

  1. Convergence in distribution (denoted Xn→dYX_n \stackrel{d}{\to}YXn​→dY) occurs when the probability distribution of XnX_nXn​ approaches that of YYY (the CDFs “line up”). Distribution-wise XnX_nXn​ and YYY are identical in the limit. However, the outcomes of a draw of XnX_nXn​ and YYY need not be identical. For example, say you have two magic coins that always land on opposite faces. (When you toss them simultaneously one always lands head and the other tail.) The distribution of both coins are equal (50% heads, 50% tails) but their realizations are not.

  2. Convergence in probability (denoted Xn→pYX_n \stackrel{p}{\to}YXn​→pY) is the stronger idea that not only do the distributions of XnX_nXn​ and YYY match in the limit, but also the realizations of any individual draws match (in the limit). Of course, for this concept to make any sense, XnX_nXn​ and YYY must come from the same draw (i.e. the same :probability space). (If the magic coins can be tossed separately you cannot talk about comparing their outcomes, only their distributions.)

Both types of convergence have :formal definitions and properties that are useful to know (e.g. convergence in probability implies convergence in distribution).

Now that we are equipped to discuss asymptotic behaviors we can introduce several useful tools:

  • The law of large numbers (LLN) states that the average of nnn independent and identically distributed (iid) random variables converges in probability to their expected value: Xnˉ→pE[Xi]\bar{X_n} \stackrel{p}{\to} E[X_i]Xn​ˉ​→pE[Xi​].

  • But how fast does an average converge? The central limit theorem says that the variance of an average shrinks at rate 1n\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}n​1​. You can think of this as saying Xnˉ→dN(E[Xi],σ2n)\bar{X_n} \stackrel{d}{\to}N(E[X_i], \frac{\sigma^2}{n})Xn​ˉ​→dN(E[Xi​],nσ2​) although technically we cannot converge onto a distribution that itself is changing with nnn and must instead write the CTL as:

n(Xnˉ−μX)→dN(0,σ2)\sqrt{n}(\bar{X_n}-\mu_X) \stackrel{d}{\to} N(0,\sigma^2) n​(Xn​ˉ​−μX​)→dN(0,σ2)

  • :O-notation helps us make arguments about rates of convergence.

  • The :Continuous Mapping Theorem (CMT), :Delta Method, and :Slutsky Theorem help derive the asymptotic behaviors of functions of random variables (e.g. g(X)→?g(X)\to ?g(X)→? or X+Y→?X+Y \to?X+Y→?)

Part 2: Statistics and estimators

Now that we’ve covered the mathematical tools of probability we are ready to discuss statistics. Recall that, much like Plato’s cave, the philosophy of frequentist statistics is that there exists a real world that we cannot observe directly. Rather, we only have a sample of that real world from which me makes inductions. We shall denote this sample as x=(x1,…,xn)x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)x=(x1​,…,xn​). Before actually observing the sample, xxx is a random variable since the value of any draw xix_ixi​ is still uncertain. As such, we will denote the before-observation sample as X=(X1,…,Xn)X = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)X=(X1​,…,Xn​).

A statistic T(X)T(X)T(X) is any function of our sample X=(X1,…,Xn)X = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)X=(X1​,…,Xn​). For example, the sample mean Xˉ\bar XXˉ is a statistic. Again, note that a statistic is a random variable because, until we actually take the sample and calculate the statistic, there is a distribution of results we might expect: the sampling distribution. Since statistics are random variables, we can discuss their properties like E[Xˉ]E[\bar X]E[Xˉ] and Var(Xˉ)Var(\bar X)Var(Xˉ).

Parameters are properties of the real (unobservable) world. Some statistics are particularly useful to estimate parameters. We call these statistics estimators and typically denote them with a hat (i.e. estimator θ^\hat \thetaθ^ for parameter θ\thetaθ). For example, sample mean Xˉ\bar XXˉ is an estimator of the real average μ\muμ (and thus, XXX and μ^\hat\muμ^​ are often used interchangeably).

What makes a “good” estimator? There are several properties to evaluate the goodness of estimators:

  • :Bias, whether over multiple experiments the average of the estimators converges onto the parameter.
  • :Efficiency, a proxy for the spread (variance) of the sampling distribution of the estimator.
  • :Consistency, whether for large sample sizes, the bias converges to 000.
  • :Asymptotic normality, whether the sampling distribution of an estimator converges onto a normal.

Note that there is a bias-efficiency tradeoff in that there is always a more efficient estimator than the unbiased one. Moreover, the :Rao-Cramer bound sets a lower bound on the variance of an estimator (this bound is based in the notion of Fisher information, a proxy for how much information the sample can convey about the distribution).

:x Why n-1?

Why should the unbiased estimator of σ2\sigma^2σ2 contain a n−1n-1n−1 term? Simplify because this is the estimator that ensures that E[s2]=σ2E[s^2] =\sigma^2E[s2]=σ2. Generating estimators and their sampling distributions

How does one find a good estimator?

  • Use the plugin estimator: the estimator that uses the same formula as that for the parameter, except using the empirical distribution instead. (e.g. sample mean μ^=1n∑i=1nXi\hat\mu=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_iμ^​=n1​∑i=1n​Xi​ to approximate μ=∫−∞∞xf(x)dx\mu=\int_{-\infty}^\infty xf(x)dxμ=∫−∞∞​xf(x)dx)

  • Use the notion of :sufficient statistics, a set of tools based on the fact that certain parameters can be entirely “captured” by a set of statistics (“sufficient statistics”). Estimators that only depend on those sufficient statistics are more efficient and less biased (or at least no worse, see :Rao-Blackwell theorem).

  • Derive the :maximum likelihood estimator (MLE). MLE estimators are consistent and asymptotically normal with variance approaching the :Rao-Cramer bound.

  • Use a :bootstrap-bias correction, especially useful for the cases where there is no unbiased estimator.

  • Use the method of moments (not covered in depth in the course).

Once one obtains the formula for an estimator (or statistic), how can one calculate its sampling distribution? There are generally four ways to do so:

  1. The distribution of simple estimators can be derived analytically. For example, it can be shown that if X1,…,XnX_1, \ldots, X_nX1​,…,Xn​ are iid random normal variables, the distribution of the sample mean Xˉ\bar XXˉ is the normal N(μ,σ2/n)N(\mu, \sigma^2 / n)N(μ,σ2/n) and the distribution of the sample variance s2s^2s2 is σ2χn−12/(n−1)\sigma^2 \chi^2_{n-1} / (n-1)σ2χn−12​/(n−1).

  2. For some estimators, the distribution can be approximated for large nnn by using asymptotic arguments (e.g. using the CLT, delta method, Slutsky theorem). For example, the central limit theorem says that for large enough nnn, Xˉ∼N(μ,σ2/n)\bar X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)Xˉ∼N(μ,σ2/n).

  3. Via a computer simulation with the :Monte Carlo method (requires knowing or assuming the true distribution).

  4. Via a computer simulation with the :non-parametric bootstrap method (doesn’t require knowing the true distribution but requires large enough nnn).

Note that many of these methods require assuming a parametric family for the true distribution (e.g. assuming we’re sampling from a Poisson distribution). As such, we might have a good estimator, but if our assumption is poor (aka. model mis-specification), we will be estimating the wrong thing. Specifically, we will be estimating a parameter θ0\theta_0θ0​ which is “pseudo-true” in the sense that it is a mix of the real and assumed distribution. Whether θ0\theta_0θ0​ is meaningful depends on the context (e.g. how far off are we), however, the variance of the estimator of θ0\theta_0θ0​ can be estimated using :White’s standard errors and this formula for variance is often preferred since it is thought to be more “robust” to model mis-specification.

:x Bootstrap bias correction

Sometimes there is no unbiased estimator. In these cases, the bootstrap bias corrector can help produce a less biased estimator: Sample b×nb \times nb×n times from our empirical observations. For every bbb calculate the “real” parameter value, average across all bbb, then subtract out the estimator to get an estimate of the systemic bias. This estimate can be removed from the estimator to make a less biased one. (This technique removes the O(1/n) bias term, but not smaller biases. It could also be removed by analytically calculating the bias.)

:x Monte Carlo

If one knows (or assumes) the unobservable distribution, one can simply simulate drawing a sample of size nnn, calculating the statistic and then repeating bbb times to get an empirical distribution for the statistic. As b→∞b \to \inftyb→∞, the empirical distribution converges on the statistic’s sampling distribution. This technique is called a Monte Carlo technique.
